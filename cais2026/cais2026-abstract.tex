\begin{abstract}
Multi-agent AI systems choose communication topologies---star, chain,
ring, fully connected---with no controlled evidence for how topology
affects behavior. We provide the first such evidence using island-model
genetic algorithms as a model system: cheap, controllable, and
structurally identical to LLM agent orchestration. Across 800+ runs
on landscapes of varying difficulty, we find three results.
(1)~Topology has a significant independent effect on behavioral
diversity ($\eta^2 = 0.17$, $p < 10^{-7}$), amplified $14\times$ on
hard problems. (2)~Two topological invariants---cycle rank and
algebraic connectivity---control diversity on different timescales.
(3)~At least ten deployed agent systems chose topologies based on
uncontrolled comparisons that confound these variables. Topology is a
first-class design variable that controls the
diversity--convergence tradeoff, and current systems are tuning it
blindly.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ===================================================================

Every multi-agent system embeds a communication topology. When
AutoGen connects agents in a round-robin group chat, that is a
topology choice. When LangGraph enforces a directed acyclic graph
(DAG) of tool calls, that is a topology choice. When CrewAI routes
tasks through a manager hub, that is a topology choice. These choices
determine which agents can exchange information---yet they are made
without controlled experimental evidence.

The stakes are not hypothetical. Dochkina~\citep{dochkina2026} ran
25,000 tasks across 256 agents and 8 coordination protocols, finding a
44\% quality spread (Cohen's $d = 1.86$) between the best and worst
topologies. Industry reports find that hierarchical multi-agent
deployments fail 36\% of the time; swarm architectures fail 68\%.
Topology is evidently doing something. The question is:
\emph{what, exactly?}

This paper provides the first controlled answer. We use island-model
genetic algorithms (GAs) as a model system---structurally identical to
agent orchestration but cheap enough for hundreds of trials per
topology. Our contribution to the CAIS track on
\emph{multi-agent systems and coordination mechanisms} is threefold:
(1)~topology has a significant, independent effect on behavioral
diversity; (2)~two distinct topological mechanisms operate on different
timescales; and (3)~the evidence base for topology choices in deployed
agent systems is systematically confounded.

\section{Island Models as Agent Systems}
\label{sec:analogy}
%% ===================================================================

An island-model GA partitions a population across sub-populations
(\emph{islands}) that evolve independently, exchanging individuals
periodically via \emph{migration} channels. The mapping to agent
orchestration is direct:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{3.2cm}p{3.8cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{GA concept} & \textbf{Agent equivalent} \\
\midrule
Island & Agent (local state, computation) \\
Migration channel & Message channel \\
Population diversity & Strategy diversity \\
Fitness function & Task performance metric \\
Migration topology & Orchestration graph \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
The analogy is structural, not metaphorical: both systems have nodes
with local state exchanging information along directed channels, with
topology determining how quickly the system converges. GAs let us run
hundreds of independent trials per topology with full experimental
control---varying one property at a time. This scale is infeasible
with stochastic LLM agents where per-run cost and variance are
prohibitive.

In both domains, \emph{diversity} measures how different the agents'
current solutions are from each other, and \emph{fitness} measures how
well the best solution performs. The core tradeoff is identical: more
diversity means more exploration but slower convergence.

\section{Definitions}
\label{sec:definitions}
%% ===================================================================

\begin{definition}[Communication topology]
A \emph{communication topology} for $n$ agents is a directed graph
$D = (V, A)$ with $|V| = n$, where each arc $(u, v) \in A$ is a
permitted channel from agent $u$ to agent $v$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Cycle rank]
The \emph{cycle rank} $\beta_1(G) = |E| - |V| + 1$ (for connected
$G$) counts independent cycles---feedback loops in the communication
structure.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Algebraic connectivity]
$\lambda_2$, the second-smallest eigenvalue of the graph Laplacian,
measures information diffusion rate: higher $\lambda_2$ means faster
mixing.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Directed cycle count]
$\kappa(D)$ counts distinct simple directed cycles in
$D$~\citep{johnson1975finding}. Unlike $\beta_1$, this depends on arc
\emph{arrangement}, not just edge count. A DAG has $\kappa = 0$.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Island models} partition a population across $n$ islands,
each running selection, crossover, and mutation independently. The best
individual from each island periodically migrates to neighbors along
the migration topology, controlling how quickly good solutions spread.

\paragraph{NK landscapes}~\citep{kauffman1993origins} are tunably
rugged fitness functions over binary strings of length $N$, where each
bit's contribution depends on $K$ other bits. $K{=}0$ gives a smooth
landscape (one optimum); increasing $K$ adds local optima, making the
problem harder. This lets us test topology effects across a controlled
difficulty range.

\section{Experimental Evidence}
\label{sec:experiments}
%% ===================================================================

We present three experiments (800+ runs total) isolating topology's
effect on multi-agent behavior.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Topology affects diversity at constant density}

A naive comparison of a 4-agent chain vs.\ a complete graph conflates
two differences: the chain has fewer connections \emph{and} fewer
cycles. Indeed, for connected graphs the cycle rank satisfies
$\beta_1 = m - n + 1$~\citep{hatcher2002algebraic}, so cycle count
and edge count are perfectly correlated. Any comparison using
undirected graphs is confounded by construction. We escape via
directed cycle count, which varies independently of density.

\paragraph{Design.}
8 directed graphs, all with $n{=}8$ nodes and 16 arcs
($\delta{=}2.0$), varying only in directed cycle count:
$\kappa \in \{0, 0, 3, 10, 14, 20, 29, 47\}$. Each serves as
migration topology for an island-model GA (50 individuals/island,
500 generations). 30 seeds per topology, 240 runs total.

\paragraph{Results.}
$F(7, 232) = 6.90$, $p = 1.85 \times 10^{-7}$, $\eta^2 = 0.17$.
More directed cycles \emph{accelerate consensus} ($r = -0.68$):
topologies with more feedback loops cause agents to converge faster,
reducing capacity to maintain alternative strategies.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Hard problems amplify the effect $14\times$}

On NK landscapes with tunable ruggedness, topology's effect scales
with difficulty:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccc@{}}
\toprule
$K$ & $\eta^2$ (diversity) & Interpretation \\
\midrule
0 (trivial) & 0.05 & Topology barely matters \\
2 (moderate) & 0.20 & Moderate effect \\
4 (hard) & 0.45 & Large effect \\
6 (very hard) & 0.69 & Topology dominates \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
The implication for agent systems: simple tasks (clear answer, easy
evaluation) are structurally smooth---topology barely matters. Complex
tasks (multiple valid strategies, ambiguous evaluation) are
structurally rugged---topology becomes the \emph{dominant} behavioral
factor. Real-world deployments overwhelmingly involve the latter.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Two mechanisms, two timescales}

Even after controlling for density, both $\beta_1$ and $\lambda_2$
could explain the effects. We separate them using \emph{iso-spectral}
graph families: same $\lambda_2$, different $\beta_1$, and vice
versa. 320 runs on NK landscapes ($K{=}4$, $N{=}20$).

Two independent mechanisms emerge on \textbf{distinct timescales}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cycle rank ($\beta_1$)}: \emph{transient} diversity,
    $\eta^2 = 0.191$ at generation~100 ($p < 0.05$), fading by
    generation~500. Feedback loops enable early exploration.
  \item \textbf{Algebraic connectivity ($\lambda_2$)}:
    \emph{persistent} diversity, $\eta^2 = 0.265$ at generation~200
    ($p = 0.003$), maintained throughout. Faster diffusion permanently
    reduces strategy diversity.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
For designers: topology controls diversity--convergence through
\emph{two independent knobs}. High $\beta_1$ with low $\lambda_2$
enables early exploration with slow convergence---ideal for complex
tasks. No current system tunes either deliberately.

\section{Implications for Agent Orchestration}
\label{sec:implications}
%% ===================================================================

\paragraph{The evidence gap.}
At least ten recent agent systems---spanning ICML, AAAI, WWW, and
NeurIPS---chose topologies without density-controlled comparisons
(Table~\ref{tab:systems}).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Agent systems whose topology choices lack controlled evidence.}
\label{tab:systems}
\footnotesize
\resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{@{}lp{3.5cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Issue} \\
\midrule
Graph-GRPO~\citep{graphgrpo2026} & Complete = worst; also max $\beta_1$ \\
OFA-MAS~\citep{ofamas2026} & Prunes density \& cycles together \\
G-Designer~\citep{gdesigner2025} & No density-controlled baseline \\
GoAgent~\citep{goagent2026} & DAG vs.\ dense alternatives \\
ARG-Designer~\citep{argdesigner2026} & DAG space only \\
AgentConductor~\citep{agentconductor2026} & Cycles never tested \\
DyTopo~\citep{dytopo2026} & DAG enforced throughout \\
HERA~\citep{hera2026} & Cycles tracked, not controlled \\
Dochkina~\citep{dochkina2026} & Density \& cycles confounded \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Safety risks.}
\emph{(1)~Misattributed robustness.} A DAG system may be reliable
because it has few channels, not because it lacks cycles. Scaling
by adding channels reintroduces cycles with unpredictable consequences.
\emph{(2)~Premature consensus.} Feedback loops accelerate convergence;
in high-stakes deliberation, this is a known failure
mode~\citep{dochkina2026}.
\emph{(3)~Error amplification.} Moran~\citep{moran2025error} reports
$17\times$ error amplification in unstructured communication. The
standard fix (impose DAG structure) simultaneously removes cycles
\emph{and} density---we cannot know which helps.

\paragraph{Recommendations.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Treat topology as a first-class variable.}
    It controls the diversity--convergence tradeoff.
  \item \textbf{Match topology to task complexity.} Simple tasks
    tolerate any topology; hard tasks demand deliberate choice.
  \item \textbf{Monitor diversity, not just accuracy.} Topology
    effects manifest in strategy diversity. Accuracy alone masks
    topology-induced failures.
  \item \textbf{Control for density.} Any claim that ``DAGs
    outperform cycles'' must hold connection count constant.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ===================================================================

Yang et al.~\citep{yang2025topology} identify topology as
\emph{the} underexplored MAS dimension, proposing nine research
directions; our work provides the controlled experiments they call for.
HERA~\citep{hera2026} is the first system to track cycle count during
topology evolution, converging on ``compact chains with strategically
preserved cycles''---consistent with our finding that moderate cycle
structure enables exploration.
Dochkina~\citep{dochkina2026} demonstrates massive topology effects
(25K tasks, $d = 1.86$) without isolating the mechanism. In
evolutionary computation, the topology--diversity link is
established~\citep{tomassini2005spatially,skolicki2005influence}, but
all prior work uses undirected graphs and does not control for density.
Capucci and Myers~\citep{capucci2026feedback} formalize cyclic
multi-agent architectures categorically, showing each cycle requires
an independent convergence guarantee---a theoretical cost complementing
our empirical benefit-side evidence.

%% ===================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ===================================================================

Communication topology is not neutral infrastructure---it is a design
variable controlling how multi-agent systems balance exploration
against convergence. Topology independently explains 17\% of diversity
variance on easy problems, rising to 69\% on hard ones, through two
mechanisms on distinct timescales. At least ten deployed systems chose
topologies based on comparisons that simultaneously varied the property
they claimed to test. For safe multi-agent deployment, topology must be
tuned deliberately, evaluated at controlled density, and monitored
through diversity metrics.
