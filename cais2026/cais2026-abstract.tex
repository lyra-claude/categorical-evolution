\begin{abstract}
Multi-agent AI systems increasingly rely on communication topologies to
coordinate behavior, yet the field lacks principled methods for reasoning
about which topological properties drive system dynamics. We prove a
fundamental methodological confound: for connected graphs with $n$ vertices
and $m$ edges, the cycle rank $\beta_1 = m - n + 1$, making cycle structure
and edge density algebraically indistinguishable in every published topology
comparison. At least ten major multi-agent systems enforce DAG
communication topologies based on confounded evidence. We escape this
identity via directed simple cycle count $\kappa(D)$, which decorrelates
from density, and present the first controlled experiment: 240 island model
runs at constant density show a significant effect of cycle structure on
population diversity ($p < 10^{-7}$, $\eta^2 = 0.17$). For AI safety, the
implication is immediate: claims about the superiority of acyclic agent
coordination are unfounded, and the field is deploying topologies whose
behavioral consequences are not understood.
\end{abstract}

%% ===================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ===================================================================

The topology of communication in multi-agent systems---which agents can
talk to which---shapes collective behavior in ways that are poorly
understood and almost never controlled. In island model evolutionary
algorithms, ring topologies maintain diversity longer than complete
graphs~\citep{tomassini2005spatially}; sparse topologies slow premature
convergence~\citep{alba2002parallel}. In LLM-based multi-agent systems,
ten or more recent architectures enforce directed acyclic graph (DAG)
communication~\citep{goagent2026,agentconductor2026,graphgrpo2026,ofamas2026,gdesigner2025,argdesigner2026,dochkina2026},
claiming that acyclic structure improves performance.
Yang et al.~\citep{yang2025topology} identify topology as \emph{the}
underexplored dimension of multi-agent system design, proposing nine
research directions---yet their analysis does not recognize the
density--cycle confound we formalize here.

From a safety perspective, this matters. Multi-agent systems are being
deployed in settings where predictable, controllable behavior is essential:
code generation pipelines, automated reasoning chains, and autonomous agent
swarms. If the field's understanding of \emph{why} certain topologies work
rests on confounded evidence, then topology choices in deployed systems are
effectively uncontrolled---designers are tuning a knob without knowing what
it does.

We show that this is precisely the situation. The cycle rank $\beta_1$ of a
connected graph with $n$ vertices and $m$ edges is $m - n + 1$: cycle
structure and density are the \emph{same variable}. Every comparison of
``cyclic vs.\ acyclic'' topologies simultaneously varies how many
communication channels exist. The DAG hegemony may reflect nothing more than
the cost savings of sparser communication.

To break this confound, we move to directed graphs, where the simple
directed cycle count $\kappa(D)$ depends on arc arrangement, not just
$(n, m)$. We construct eight graph families at constant density and run
240 controlled experiments. The result: cycle structure has an independent,
significant effect on diversity dynamics---an effect invisible to every
prior experimental design.

%% ===================================================================
\section{The Density--Cycle Confound}
\label{sec:confound}
%% ===================================================================

\begin{theorem}[Cycle rank--density identity]
\label{thm:confound}
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a connected graph with $|V| = n$ and $|E| = m$. Then
$\beta_1(G) = m - n + 1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
A spanning tree of $G$ uses $n - 1$ edges. Each remaining edge closes
exactly one independent cycle. The result follows from the Euler
characteristic $\chi = n - m = 1 - \beta_1$ for connected 1-dimensional
simplicial complexes~\citep{hatcher2002algebraic}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:pearson}
For connected graphs with fixed $n$, $\beta_1$ and $m$ differ by the
constant $n - 1$. Pearson correlation is affine-invariant, so
$\rho(\beta_1, Y) = \rho(m, Y)$ for any outcome variable~$Y$.
\end{corollary}

The methodological consequence: \emph{any experiment comparing connected
topologies on a fixed node count cannot distinguish ``cycles affect
performance'' from ``edge count affects performance.''} This is not a subtle
statistical concern---it is a mathematical identity.

\paragraph{Scope of the confound.}
Table~\ref{tab:confound} surveys ten systems that enforce DAG
communication. In every case, the comparison that motivates the DAG
constraint simultaneously varies cycle structure and density. GoAgent's
CIB objective~\citep{goagent2026} compares DAGs to dense baselines;
Graph-GRPO~\citep{graphgrpo2026} learns DAGs and evaluates against
complete graphs (maximum density \emph{and} maximum $\beta_1$); Dochkina
et al.~\citep{dochkina2026}, the most rigorous study (25,000+ runs),
confounds sparsity and acyclicity throughout.

\paragraph{The safety implication.}
If we cannot distinguish ``fewer cycles help'' from ``fewer edges help,''
then the DAG constraint in deployed systems may be discarding useful
feedback pathways (cycles that enable error correction and redundancy) while
the actual benefit comes from reduced communication overhead. A system
designed for safety through acyclicity may be safe only because it is
sparse---and adding edges for robustness would reintroduce cycles, with
unknown behavioral consequences.

\section{The Directed Escape}
\label{sec:escape}
%% ===================================================================

Theorem~\ref{thm:confound} applies to the homological cycle rank. For
directed graphs, the \emph{simple directed cycle count} $\kappa(D)$---the
number of elementary directed cycles---is combinatorial, not homological.
It depends on the specific arc arrangement, not just on $(n, m)$.

We constructed eight directed graph families, all with $n = 8$ vertices and
$m = 16$ arcs (density $m/n = 2.0$ held constant). The families span
$\kappa$ from 0 (pure DAGs) to 47 (ring with skip-2 connections). Among
strongly connected members, $\kappa$ ranges from 10 to 47---a $4.7\times$
variation at strictly constant density. Table~\ref{tab:families} summarizes.

\section{Experiment: Constant-Density GA}
\label{sec:experiment}
%% ===================================================================

\paragraph{Setup.}
Each of the 8 graph families serves as an island model GA migration
topology. Each vertex hosts a subpopulation of 50 individuals (400 total).
Fitness function: OneMax on 100-bit strings ($f(\mathbf{x}) =
L^{-1}\sum_k x_k$). Selection: tournament ($k=3$). Crossover: uniform
($p = 0.5$/locus). Mutation: bit-flip at rate $1/L$. Every 10 generations,
the 5 fittest individuals migrate along outgoing arcs, replacing the 5
weakest. All families share density $16/8 = 2.0$. Each topology runs with
30 random seeds (240 runs total, 500 generations each).

\paragraph{Diversity metric.}
Mean pairwise normalized Hamming distance across all 400 individuals,
estimated from 20 randomly sampled pairs.

\paragraph{Statistical analysis.}
One-way ANOVA with topology as factor; Kruskal--Wallis for non-parametric
confirmation; Pearson $r$ between $\kappa$ and metrics at generations 30,
60, and 100. By construction, $r(\text{density}, Y) = 0$ identically for
all outcomes $Y$.

\subsection{Results}

\paragraph{Main effect.}
One-way ANOVA at generation 30: $F(7, 232) = 6.90$, $p = 1.85 \times
10^{-7}$. Kruskal--Wallis corroborates: $H = 38.53$, $p = 2.40 \times
10^{-6}$. Effect sizes: $\eta^2 = 0.17$ for diversity, $\eta^2 = 0.24$
for fitness---medium-to-large by Cohen's conventions.

\paragraph{Cycle count predicts diversity.}
Pearson $r = -0.681$ between $\kappa$ and mean diversity at generation 30
($p = 0.063$ on 6 df; marginal on 8 topology-level points, but
corroborated by the 240-run ANOVA). Higher cycle count correlates with
faster diversity loss: more feedback loops accelerate the spread of
dominant alleles across islands.

\paragraph{Transience.}
The effect peaks at generations 20--50 and decays by generation 100
($|r| < 0.10$, $\eta^2 = 0.002$). OneMax is unimodal, so all topologies
converge eventually. $\kappa$ affects the \emph{rate} of convergence, not
the outcome. DAG families preserve diversity longest; \texttt{ring-skip2}
($\kappa = 47$) converges fastest.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Even on the simplest possible fitness landscape, directed cycle structure
independently explains 17\% of diversity variance and 24\% of fitness
variance during the critical early phase. This is the first demonstration
that cycles affect dynamics \emph{at constant density}.

\section{Implications for AI Safety}
\label{sec:safety}
%% ===================================================================

\paragraph{Uncontrolled topology is uncontrolled behavior.}
Our theorem shows that the field's evidence base for topology design is
confounded. Systems deployed with DAG topologies may behave predictably
not because they lack cycles, but because they are sparse. If a system
is scaled up by adding communication channels (e.g., for fault tolerance
or richer coordination), cycles will inevitably appear---and their
behavioral effects, as our experiment demonstrates, are real and
significant.

\paragraph{Diversity as a safety mechanism.}
In evolutionary computation, diversity maintenance prevents premature
convergence to suboptimal solutions. In multi-agent AI, the analog is
\emph{behavioral diversity}: the system's ability to explore multiple
solution strategies rather than collapsing to a single mode.
High-$\kappa$ topologies accelerate diversity loss---equivalent to the
system ``making up its mind'' faster. Whether this is desirable depends
on the task: for safety-critical applications where premature consensus
is dangerous (e.g., multi-agent deliberation for high-stakes decisions),
understanding the topology--diversity relationship is essential.

\paragraph{The landscape matters.}
A pilot study on NK landscapes~\citep{kauffman1993origins} (rugged,
multi-peaked fitness functions) shows that topology effects scale
dramatically with problem difficulty: $\eta^2$ increases from 0.05
($K = 0$, smooth) to 0.69 ($K = 6$, rugged)---a $14\times$
amplification. Real-world problems facing deployed AI systems are
closer to $K = 6$ than to OneMax. The moderate effects we observe on
a toy problem are likely lower bounds on what matters in practice.

\paragraph{Toward principled topology design.}
We propose that AI safety frameworks for multi-agent systems must include
topology as a first-class design variable, with three requirements:
(1)~density and cycle structure must be controlled independently;
(2)~diversity dynamics, not just task performance, must be monitored;
(3)~topology choices must be justified against constant-density baselines,
not confounded comparisons.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ===================================================================

HERA~\citep{hera2026} is the first system to track cycle count during
topology evolution, converging to ``compact chains with strategically
preserved cycles.'' Puppeteer~\citep{puppeteer2025} discovers cyclic
structures under optimization pressure, outperforming DAG-constrained
baselines. Both provide evidence that cycles are functionally useful, but
neither controls for density. Our theorem explains why their results are
promising but not conclusive, and our experimental design provides the
controlled condition they lack.

In evolutionary computation, the topology--diversity relationship is
well-established~\citep{tomassini2005spatially,skolicki2005influence}, but
all prior work uses undirected graphs and therefore falls under the
confound. The directed cycle count $\kappa(D)$ as an experimental variable
is, to our knowledge, novel.

%% ===================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ===================================================================

For connected graphs, cycle rank equals edge count minus a constant. This
identity means that every published comparison of cyclic vs.\ acyclic
multi-agent topologies is confounded. We escape the confound via directed
simple cycle count, which varies independently of density, and demonstrate
a significant independent effect ($p < 10^{-7}$) across 240 controlled
runs. Preliminary NK landscape results suggest $14\times$ amplification on
harder problems.

For AI safety, the message is: the field is building multi-agent systems
with uncontrolled topology. The DAG hegemony rests on confounded evidence.
Before we can reason about the safety properties of agent communication
structures, we must first ensure we are measuring the right thing. This
paper provides the theorem that exposes the problem and the experimental
methodology that begins to solve it.
