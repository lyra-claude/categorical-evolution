\begin{abstract}
A genetic algorithm (GA) maintains a \emph{population} of candidate
solutions (genomes), improving them through mutation and crossover
guided by a fitness function; an \emph{island model} partitions this
population across sub-populations (islands) that evolve independently
and periodically exchange individuals along a communication topology.
This paper shows that the same topology effects govern LLM agent
orchestration: agents correspond to islands, message channels to
migration channels, and output diversity---measured as mean pairwise
cosine distance on sentence embeddings---plays the role of population
diversity.

Multi-agent AI systems choose communication topologies---star, chain,
ring, fully connected---with no controlled evidence for how topology
affects behavior. We provide five lines of evidence across 970 GA
runs and 3,200 LLM API calls. First, we prove that cycle rank and
edge density are algebraically confounded in undirected graphs,
invalidating density-uncontrolled topology comparisons (Theorem~1).
Second, using directed graphs that escape this confound (240~runs),
topology independently explains 17\% of diversity variance
($p < 10^{-7}$). Third, an NK dose-response sweep (250~runs) shows
the effect scales $14\times$ from smooth to rugged landscapes. Fourth,
a ring-vs-star comparison at constant cycle rank (160~runs) shows
degree distribution contributes at most $\eta^2 \approx 0.13$ within
a single $\beta_1$ class. Fifth, a bridge experiment (320~runs) with
iso-spectral families decomposes the topology signal: cycle rank
($\beta_1$) drives transient diversity ($\eta^2 = 0.191$ at
generation~100, fading by~500), while algebraic connectivity
($\lambda_2$) drives persistent diversity (cross-family $\eta^2 =
0.265$ at generation~200). Transfer to LLM agents: a coupling-dependent
sign flip means one instruction reverses which topology produces the
most diverse output ($\eta^2 = 0.291$, $p < 10^{-19}$). At least nine
published systems chose topologies without the controlled comparisons
our confound theorem demands. Topology is a first-class design
variable; current systems are tuning it blindly.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ===================================================================

Every multi-agent system embeds a communication topology. AutoGen uses
round-robin group chat. LangGraph builds DAG pipelines. CrewAI deploys
manager hubs. These are not neutral infrastructure choices---they
determine which agents can see which outputs, shaping the
diversity--convergence tradeoff that governs system behavior. Yet
these choices are made without controlled evidence.

The costs of acyclicity are not merely empirical.
Ao et al.~\cite{ao2026reliability} prove (Proposition~6) that
DAG-constrained multi-agent systems are decision-theoretically
dominated by a centralized Bayes decision maker, establishing a
formal cost to acyclicity. Our work investigates whether introducing
cycles ($\beta_1 > 0$) recovers performance by restoring the
feedback that DAG constraints eliminate.

Dochkina~\cite{dochkina2026} finds a 44\% quality spread ($d = 1.86$)
across 25K~tasks and 8~protocols. Moran~\cite{moran2025error} reports, in an informal practitioner
analysis, $17\times$ error amplification in unstructured communication.
Yang et al.~\cite{yang2025topology} call topology \emph{the}
underexplored MAS dimension. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2026kregular} show
$K$-regular graphs maximize coordination ($\beta_{\min} \sim 1/K$,
where $\beta_{\min}$ is the minimum algebraic connectivity threshold
for consensus).

But \emph{what, exactly}, is topology doing? Comparing a chain
($\beta_1 = 0$) to a complete graph simultaneously varies cycle rank,
density, diameter, and spectral gap. At least nine published
systems---spanning ICML, AAAI, WWW, NeurIPS, and AAMAS---have chosen
topologies without density-controlled comparisons
(Table~\ref{tab:evidence-gap}):
Graph-GRPO~\cite{graphgrpo2026} reports that complete graphs perform
worst, but that comparison confounds density and cycle rank.
OFA-MAS~\cite{ofamas2026} prunes topology jointly on both dimensions.
G-Designer~\cite{gdesigner2025} searches over topologies with no
density-fixed baseline.
GoAgent~\cite{goagent2026} compares DAGs to dense alternatives without
controlling edge count.
ARG-Designer~\cite{argdesigner2026} and
AgentConductor~\cite{agentconductor2026} restrict search to DAG
space and never test cyclic alternatives.
DyTopo~\cite{dytopo2026} enforces acyclicity throughout; its dynamic
topology shifts conflate structure and time.
HERA~\cite{hera2026} tracks cycle count but not density.
Dochkina~\cite{dochkina2026}'s 25K-task study reports a 44\% quality
spread across 8 protocols, but the protocols differ simultaneously on
density, cycle structure, and spectral gap.
Our confound theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:confound}) shows all nine of
these comparisons are fundamentally uninterpretable.

We provide the first controlled answer, bridging from a model system
(genetic algorithms) to real LLM agents. Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Confound Theorem} (\S\ref{sec:definitions}): cycle rank
    and density are algebraically identical for undirected graphs
    ($\beta_1 = |E| - n + 1$), making density-uncontrolled comparisons
    uninterpretable. Directed cycle count $\kappa$ escapes this confound.

  \item \textbf{Controlled GA experiments} (\S\S\ref{sec:exp1}--\ref{sec:exp4}):
    970 runs establish that topology independently determines
    behavioral diversity, the effect scales $14\times$ with problem
    difficulty, cycle rank matters more than degree distribution, and
    two invariants ($\beta_1$, $\lambda_2$) control diversity at
    different timescales.

  \item \textbf{LLM validation} (\S\ref{sec:exp5}): 3,200
    GPT-4o-mini API calls reveal a coupling-dependent sign
    flip---one instruction change reverses the diversity ordering
    ($\eta^2 = 0.291$).

  \item \textbf{Invariant hierarchy} (\S\ref{sec:discussion}):
    three invariants---$\kappa$, $\beta_1$, $\lambda_2$---operating at
    different timescales provide a principled topology selection
    framework. Nine recent systems lack the controlled comparisons our
    theorem demands.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background: Island Models as Agent Systems}
\label{sec:background}
%% ===================================================================

An island-model genetic algorithm (GA) partitions a population across
sub-populations (\emph{islands}) that evolve independently, exchanging
individuals periodically via \emph{migration}
channels~\cite{tomassini2005spatially}. The mapping to agent
orchestration is direct at the graph level (Table~\ref{tab:mapping}).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Island-model GA concepts and their agent-system equivalents.
The mapping is exact at the graph level (rows 1--5) and analogical at
the node level (row 6). Our object of study---the
diversity--topology relationship---is a graph-level property in both
systems.}
\label{tab:mapping}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{GA Concept} & \textbf{Agent Equivalent} & \textbf{Level} \\
\midrule
Island & Agent (local state, computation) & Node \\
Migration channel & Message channel & Edge \\
Migration topology & Orchestration graph & Graph \\
Population diversity & Solution diversity & Graph metric \\
Fitness function & Task performance metric & Evaluation \\
\midrule
Population (many individuals) & Single output per agent & Node \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Where the analogy holds and breaks.}
Both systems are nodes with local state exchanging information along
directed channels; the critical shared structure is the topology and
its effect on aggregate diversity. The mapping breaks at the mechanism
level: GA migration is blind copying of genotypes, while agent
communication involves semantic understanding and selective
incorporation---precisely what makes the sign-flip (Section~\ref{sec:exp5})
interesting. GAs provide cheap, reproducible experimental control,
letting us sweep parameter spaces before investing in LLM validation.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\textbf{GA diversity} is measured as mean pairwise Hamming distance
between all individuals across all islands (normalized by string
length), capturing the fraction of the search space the population
spans. \textbf{GA fitness} is the fitness of the best individual found
across all islands. \textbf{Agent diversity} is mean pairwise cosine
distance on sentence embeddings (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) of agent outputs.
\textbf{Agent fitness} is task performance (test pass rate).

\section{Definitions and the Confound Theorem}
\label{sec:definitions}
%% ===================================================================

We define the graph-theoretic quantities that our experiments control
and measure, together with their agent-system interpretations.

\begin{definition}[Communication topology]
\label{def:topology}
A \emph{communication topology} for $n$~agents is a directed graph
$D = (V, A)$ with $|V| = n$, where each arc $(u, v) \in A$ is a
permitted message channel from agent~$u$ to agent~$v$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
\textit{Agent interpretation:} Arc $(u, v)$: agent~$v$ receives
agent~$u$'s output in its context window.

\begin{definition}[Cycle rank]
\label{def:cyclerank}
The \emph{cycle rank} (first Betti number) of a connected undirected
graph $G = (V, E)$ is
\begin{equation}
  \beta_1(G) = |E| - |V| + 1.
\end{equation}
It counts the number of independent cycles in
$G$~\cite{hatcher2002algebraic}---equivalently, the minimum number of
edges whose removal makes $G$ a tree.
\end{definition}

\noindent
\textit{Agent interpretation:} $\beta_1$ counts independent feedback
loops. A tree ($\beta_1 = 0$) has no feedback; each additional cycle
lets agents respond to downstream effects. Capucci and
Myers~\cite{capucci2026feedback} formalize the cost: each cycle
requires an independent convergence guarantee.

\begin{definition}[Algebraic connectivity]
\label{def:lambda2}
The \emph{algebraic connectivity} $\lambda_2(G)$ is the second-smallest
eigenvalue of the Laplacian matrix $L = D - A$ of the undirected
graph underlying~$D$, where $D$ is the degree matrix and $A$ the
adjacency matrix. $\lambda_2 > 0$ iff $G$ is
connected~\cite{fiedler1973algebraic}.
\end{definition}

\noindent
\textit{Agent interpretation:} $\lambda_2$ governs propagation rate.
High $\lambda_2$: fast consensus; low $\lambda_2$: slower mixing,
more local diversity.

\begin{definition}[Directed cycle count]
\label{def:kappa}
$\kappa(D)$ counts the number of distinct simple directed cycles in a
directed graph $D$~\cite{johnson1975finding}. Unlike $\beta_1$, this
depends on arc \emph{arrangement}, not just edge count. A DAG has
$\kappa = 0$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
\textit{Agent interpretation:} $\kappa$ counts ways information
circulates back to its source. Two digraphs with identical arc count
can have $\kappa \in [0, 47]$---different feedback at identical bandwidth.

\paragraph{Timescale hierarchy.}
\label{rem:hierarchy}
These three invariants operate at different timescales in our
experiments: directed cycle count $\kappa$ determines the
\emph{arrangement} of feedback (Experiment~1), cycle rank $\beta_1$
determines the \emph{amount} of transient diversity
(Experiment~4, generation~100), and algebraic connectivity
$\lambda_2$ determines the \emph{rate} of long-term convergence
(Experiment~4, generation~200+). This suggests a hierarchy:
$\kappa \to \beta_1 \to \lambda_2$, from fast to slow.

We now state the central theoretical result that motivates our
experimental design.

\begin{thm}[Density--Cycle Confound]
\label{thm:confound}
For any connected undirected graph $G$ on $n$ vertices,
$\beta_1(G) = |E| - n + 1$. Therefore, cycle rank and edge density
are not independent variables: every comparison that varies density
simultaneously varies cycle rank, and vice versa.
\end{thm}

\begin{proof}
By elementary algebraic topology~\cite{hatcher2002algebraic}, the
first Betti number of a connected graph equals the number of edges
minus the number of vertices plus the number of connected components.
For a connected graph, this gives $\beta_1 = |E| - |V| + 1 = |E| - n + 1$.
Since edge density $\delta = |E| / \binom{n}{2}$, we have
$\beta_1 = \delta \binom{n}{2} - n + 1$, a linear function of
$\delta$ at fixed $n$. Varying one necessarily varies the other.
\end{proof}

\noindent
\textit{Consequence:} Any comparison varying edge count simultaneously
varies cycle rank. To isolate cycle structure, one must hold density
constant (Experiments~1, 3, 4) or use directed graphs where $\kappa$
varies independently of arc count (Experiment~1). Nine recent systems
fail to make this distinction (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\paragraph{NK landscapes.}
NK landscapes~\cite{kauffman1993origins} provide tunably rugged
fitness over binary strings of length~$N$. Each bit depends on
$K$~others; $K = 0$ is smooth, increasing $K$ adds local optima. We
use $N = 20$. NK landscapes proxy task complexity: low $K$ =
simple, high $K$ = multiple valid strategies.

\section{Experiment 1: Directed Cycles at Constant Density}
\label{sec:exp1}
%% ===================================================================

The confound theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:confound}) shows that
undirected graph comparisons cannot isolate cycle structure from
density. We escape this confound using directed graphs, where the
directed cycle count $\kappa$ varies independently of arc count.

\paragraph{Design.}
Eight directed graphs, all with $n = 8$ vertices and exactly 16~arcs
(constant density $\delta = 2.0$ arcs per vertex), varying only in
directed cycle count:
$\kappa \in \{0, 0, 3, 10, 14, 20, 29, 47\}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:iso-dense}). The two $\kappa = 0$
graphs (DAGs) serve as replicated controls. Island-model GA: 50
individuals per island, 500~generations, OneMax fitness function
($K = 0$), 30~seeds per topology, 240~runs total. Genetic operators:
uniform crossover, bit-flip mutation at rate $1/N = 0.05$
(genome length $N = 20$), tournament selection (size~3). Migration:
10\% of island population (5~individuals) every 10~generations.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{iso-dense-graphs.pdf}
\caption{Eight directed graphs with identical density (16 arcs, 8 nodes)
but varying directed cycle count $\kappa$ from 0 to 47. Despite identical
bandwidth, cycle structure alone drives the diversity effect.}
\label{fig:iso-dense}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Results.}
One-way ANOVA: $F(7, 232) = 6.90$, $p = 1.85 \times 10^{-7}$,
$\eta^2 = 0.17$. Topology explains 17\% of diversity variance at
constant density. The correlation between directed cycle count and
final diversity is $r = -0.68$: more directed cycles \emph{accelerate
consensus}. Topologies with more feedback loops cause populations to
converge faster, reducing the system's capacity to maintain
alternative solutions.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Even on a trivial landscape ($K = 0$), cycle structure alone produces
$\eta^2 = 0.17$. More feedback pathways accelerate mixing and convergence.

\section{Experiment 2: NK Dose-Response}
\label{sec:exp2}
%% ===================================================================

Experiment~1 establishes that topology affects diversity on a trivial
problem. Real agent deployments involve complex tasks with multiple
valid approaches. Do topology effects grow or shrink as problems get
harder?

\paragraph{Design.}
NK landscapes with $K \in \{0, 2, 4, 6\}$ (four ruggedness levels),
topologies selected to span the $\kappa$ range from Experiment~1,
10--20 seeds per condition, 250~runs total. All other parameters
(population size, migration rate, number of generations) identical to
Experiment~1.

\paragraph{Results.}
Topology's diversity effect scales monotonically with landscape
ruggedness (Table~\ref{tab:nk-dose}, Figure~\ref{fig:eta-vs-k}):

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Topology effect size ($\eta^2$) on diversity scales
$14\times$ from smooth to rugged NK landscapes. Simple tasks show
negligible topology effects; complex tasks are dominated by topology.}
\label{tab:nk-dose}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccll@{}}
\toprule
$K$ & $\eta^2$ (diversity) & $p$-value & Interpretation \\
\midrule
0 (smooth) & 0.05 & $> 0.05$ & Topology barely matters \\
2 (moderate) & 0.20 & $< 0.01$ & Moderate effect \\
4 (rugged) & 0.45 & $< 10^{-4}$ & Large effect \\
6 (very rugged) & 0.69 & $< 10^{-6}$ & Topology dominates \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{eta-squared-vs-k.pdf}
\caption{Topology effect size ($\eta^2$ for diversity) as a function
of NK landscape ruggedness $K$. The effect scales $14\times$ from $K=0$ to $K=6$,
demonstrating that complex tasks amplify topology's influence.}
\label{fig:eta-vs-k}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Causal chain.}
The mechanism proceeds in stages: topology $\to$ diversity $\to$ mean
fitness, with minimal direct effect on best fitness. At $K = 6$:
topology explains $\eta^2 \approx 0.69$ of diversity variance,
diversity explains $\eta^2 \approx 0.35$ of mean fitness variance,
but topology's direct effect on best fitness is only
$\eta^2 \approx 0.02$. Topology acts primarily through the
diversity channel, not by directly improving or degrading the best
solution found.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Simple tasks have one optimum; complex tasks have multiple competing
strategies, and topology determines whether the system explores many
or collapses to one. The $14\times$ amplification suggests topology
effects in production systems are far larger than simple benchmarks
indicate---consistent with Artis et al.~\cite{artis2026heterogeneity}:
environments that permit heterogeneity reward it, and topology
preserves or destroys it.

\section{Experiment 3: Ring vs Star at Constant $\beta_1$}
\label{sec:exp3}
%% ===================================================================

Within a single $\beta_1$ class, how much does edge \emph{arrangement}
matter? A plain $n$-node ring has $n$ edges ($\beta_1 = 1$); a plain
$n$-node star has $n - 1$ edges ($\beta_1 = 0$). To hold both $\beta_1$
and $|E|$ constant while varying degree distribution, we augment the
star with one leaf-to-leaf edge, yielding $|E| = n$ and $\beta_1 = 1$
for both---isolating degree distribution (uniform 2-regular vs.\
hub-and-spoke) at fixed cycle rank and edge count.

\paragraph{Design.}
Two topologies on $n = 8$ nodes: ring (2-regular, diameter~4,
$\lambda_2 \approx 0.586$) and augmented star (star $+$ one
leaf-to-leaf edge; irregular, diameter~2 hub to leaf,
$\lambda_2 = 1.0$). Both have $\beta_1 = 1$ and $|E| = 8$. NK
landscapes with $K \in \{0, 2, 4, 6\}$, 20~seeds per condition,
160~runs total (80~ring, 80~star). Island-model GA: 50 individuals
per island, 500~generations.

\paragraph{Results.}
The ring-vs-star effect is small compared to the cross-$\beta_1$
effects in Experiments~1--2 (Table~\ref{tab:ring-star}, Figure~\ref{fig:ring-vs-star}). At
generation~500:

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Ring vs star comparison at constant $\beta_1 = 1$. Effect
sizes are small ($\eta^2 < 0.15$) and non-significant after
generation~100, contrasting with $\eta^2 = 0.17$--$0.69$ across
$\beta_1$ classes in Experiments~1--2. Cycle rank matters more than
degree distribution.}
\label{tab:ring-star}
\begin{tabular}{@{}cccccl@{}}
\toprule
$K$ & $\eta^2$ (diversity) & $p$ & Ring mean & Star mean & Winner \\
\midrule
0 & 0.014 & 0.59 & 0.046 & 0.047 & --- \\
2 & 0.132 & 0.22 & 0.062 & 0.045 & ring \\
4 & 0.077 & 0.10 & 0.148 & 0.103 & ring \\
6 & 0.080 & 0.12 & 0.249 & 0.192 & ring \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{ring-vs-star-diversity.pdf}
\caption{Ring vs star diversity at generation 500 across NK landscape
ruggedness. Both topologies have $\beta_1 = 1$. The ring consistently

\paragraph{Temporal dynamics.}
At $K = 6$, generation~100: $\eta^2 = 0.161$ ($p = 0.02$, the only
significant comparison), fading to $0.080$ by generation~500. The
ring's lower $\lambda_2$ preserves diversity transiently.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The star's higher $\lambda_2$ ($1.0$ vs $0.586$) enables faster
consensus, but the effect ($\eta^2 \leq 0.13$, non-significant after
generation~100) is small relative to cross-$\beta_1$ effects
($\eta^2 = 0.17$--$0.69$). \textbf{Cycle rank is the primary
invariant}; degree distribution is secondary.

\section{Experiment 4: Bridge --- $\beta_1$ vs $\lambda_2$ Decomposition}
\label{sec:exp4}
%% ===================================================================

$\beta_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are correlated. We disentangle them using
\emph{iso-spectral graph families}: graphs sharing $\lambda_2$ but
differing in $\beta_1$. Within a family, diversity differences are
driven by $\beta_1$; across families at matched $\beta_1$, by
$\lambda_2$.

\paragraph{Design.}
Two families of four graphs each, all on $n = 8$ vertices:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Cycle family} ($\lambda_2 \approx 0.586$): 8-node
    ring ($\beta_1 = 1$), ring + 1~chord ($\beta_1 = 2$), ring +
    2~chords ($\beta_1 = 3$), ring + 3~chords ($\beta_1 = 4$). Adding
    chords increases $\beta_1$ while preserving the spectral gap via
    careful edge selection.

  \item \textbf{Star family} ($\lambda_2 = 1.0$): star with one
    leaf-to-leaf edge ($\beta_1 = 1$), star + 1~leaf-edge ($\beta_1 = 2$), star +
    2~leaf-edges ($\beta_1 = 3$), star + 3~leaf-edges ($\beta_1 = 4$).
    Adding leaf-to-leaf connections increases $\beta_1$ while the
    hub structure preserves $\lambda_2$.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Two NK landscapes: $K = 0$ (smooth, negative control) and $K = 4$
(rugged). 20~seeds per condition. Total: $8 \text{ topologies} \times
2 \text{ landscapes} \times 20 \text{ seeds} = 320$~runs.

\paragraph{Results.}
The experiment reveals two independent mechanisms operating at
different timescales (Table~\ref{tab:bridge}, Figure~\ref{fig:bridge-diversity}):

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Bridge experiment: two-way ANOVA decomposing diversity
variance into $\beta_1$ (within-family) and $\lambda_2$
(cross-family) effects on NK $K = 4$ landscapes. $\beta_1$ peaks
early and fades; $\lambda_2$ emerges later and persists.}
\label{tab:bridge}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
\toprule
Generation & $\eta^2_{\beta_1}$ & $p_{\beta_1}$ &
$\eta^2_{\lambda_2}$ & $p_{\lambda_2}$ \\
\midrule
50  & 0.037 & 0.11 & 0.001 & 0.76 \\
100 & 0.057 & 0.03 & 0.006 & 0.41 \\
200 & 0.045 & 0.07 & 0.112 & $< 0.001$ \\
500 & 0.014 & 0.44 & 0.066 & 0.005 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{bridge-diversity-generations.pdf}
\hfill
\includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{bridge-eta2-temporal.pdf}
\caption{Bridge experiment. \textbf{(a)}~\emph{Left panel:} diversity
trajectories for the two iso-spectral families. Within each family
(constant $\lambda_2$, varying $\beta_1$), higher $\beta_1$ reduces
early diversity (transient). Across families (different $\lambda_2$,
same $\beta_1$), higher $\lambda_2$ converges faster (persistent).
\textbf{(b)}~\emph{Right panel:} temporal $\eta^2$ decomposition.
$\beta_1$ peaks at generation~100 and fades; $\lambda_2$ emerges at
generation~200 and persists. Two invariants, two timescales.}
\label{fig:bridge-diversity}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{$\beta_1$ drives transient diversity.}
Within the cycle family ($\lambda_2 = 0.586$), $\beta_1$ explains
$\eta^2 = 0.191$ of diversity variance at generation~100
($F(3, 76) = 5.997$, $p = 0.001$) but fades to $\eta^2 = 0.067$ by
generation~500 ($p = 0.15$). Higher $\beta_1$ means more feedback
loops, which accelerate the initial mixing of information---but this
advantage is transient. The star family ($\lambda_2 = 1.0$) shows a
similar but weaker within-family $\beta_1$ effect ($\eta^2 = 0.092$
at generation~100, $p = 0.06$).

\paragraph{$\lambda_2$ drives persistent diversity.}
Across families at matched $\beta_1$, the family effect
($\lambda_2$) is negligible at generation~50 ($\eta^2 = 0.001$) but
emerges strongly at generation~200 ($\eta^2 = 0.112$,
$p < 0.001$) and persists through generation~500 ($\eta^2 = 0.066$,
$p = 0.005$). The cross-family analysis at $\beta_1 = 1$ confirms:
$\eta^2 = 0.265$ at generation~200 ($p = 0.003$), meaning the star
family ($\lambda_2 = 1.0$) converges to lower diversity than the
cycle family ($\lambda_2 = 0.586$) at the same cycle rank.

\paragraph{Negative control and interpretation.}
On the smooth landscape ($K = 0$), both effects are negligible
($\eta^2 < 0.02$), confirming both mechanisms require ruggedness---consistent with Experiment~2.
$\beta_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are independent: $\beta_1$ determines
initial mixing speed (transient, first ${\sim}100$~generations);
$\lambda_2$ governs long-term consensus (persistent, generation~200+).
For agent systems: \emph{cycle rank controls the exploration window};
\emph{algebraic connectivity controls convergence rate}.

\section{Experiment 5: LLM Sign-Flip}
\label{sec:exp5}
%% ===================================================================

The GA experiments (Sections~\ref{sec:exp1}--\ref{sec:exp4}) establish
that topology controls diversity through cycle rank and spectral gap.
But does this transfer to LLM agents? The node-level differences
(populations vs.\ single outputs, genetic operators vs.\ language
generation) are substantial. We test directly.

\paragraph{Coding tasks.}
We use eight algorithmic coding tasks of varying multimodality
(number of distinct valid implementations), chosen to span from
unimodal to highly multimodal:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Shortest path:} return the shortest path between two
    nodes in a weighted graph (Dijkstra, Bellman-Ford, or BFS depending
    on edge weights).
  \item \textbf{Kth largest:} find the $k$-th largest element in an
    unsorted array (quickselect, heap, or sort-based).
  \item \textbf{Rate limiter:} implement a token-bucket or sliding-window
    rate limiter for API requests (fixed window, sliding log, token
    bucket, leaky bucket).
  \item \textbf{JSON parser:} parse a JSON string into a native data
    structure without using built-in JSON libraries (recursive descent
    or iterative stack-based).
  \item \textbf{LRU cache:} implement a least-recently-used cache with
    $O(1)$ get and put (doubly linked list + hash map, or ordered dict).
  \item \textbf{Regex matcher:} implement basic regular expression
    matching supporting \texttt{.} and \texttt{*} (dynamic programming,
    recursive backtracking, or NFA).
  \item \textbf{Expression evaluator:} evaluate an arithmetic expression
    string with operator precedence (recursive descent, Pratt parser,
    or stack-based shunting-yard).
  \item \textbf{Bloom filter:} implement a Bloom filter for set
    membership testing (essentially one canonical implementation: bit
    array + $k$ hash functions).
\end{itemize}

\noindent
The Bloom filter is intentionally unimodal; the expression evaluator
and JSON parser are multimodal. Task pass/fail is determined by a
hidden test suite executed against agent outputs.

\paragraph{Design.}
Five GPT-4o-mini agents, eight coding tasks, four topologies (none
(agents receive no neighbours' outputs), ring, star, fully connected),
10~seeds per condition. Two coupling conditions, 1,600 API calls each
(3,200 total):

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Negative coupling:} each agent is instructed to use a
    \emph{different} algorithm from those visible in its context.
  \item \textbf{Positive coupling:} each agent is instructed to
    ``build on and improve'' neighbors' solutions.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
Diversity is measured as mean pairwise cosine distance on sentence
embeddings (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) of agent outputs.

\paragraph{Metric validation.}
Under ``none'' topology, five independent agents produce
near-identical outputs (mean diversity 0.090, $\sigma \approx 0.01$),
confirming LLMs' peaked prior and the metric's sensitivity.

\paragraph{Results: negative coupling.}
The diversity ordering \textbf{inverts} from the GA result
(Table~\ref{tab:signflip}, Figure~\ref{fig:llm-heatmap}):

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Topology--diversity ordering inverts between GAs and LLM
agents. The sign depends on whether coupling merges solutions
(GA-like) or constrains them (``be different''). A single instruction
change flips the ordering.}
\label{tab:signflip}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Diversity ordering} & \textbf{Statistic} \\
\midrule
GA & none $>$ ring $>$ FC & $W = 1.0$ (6 domains) \\
LLM (negative) & FC $>$ ring $\approx$ star $>$ none &
  $W = 0.91$ (6/8 tasks) \\
LLM (positive) & none $>$ star $\approx$ ring $>$ FC &
  $\eta^2 = 0.291$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Six of eight tasks show the inverted ordering cleanly (Kruskal-Wallis
$p < 10^{-6}$; Cohen's $d$ from $-14.0$ to $-1.9$). Two exceptions
are informative:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Bloom filter} ($d \approx 0$): a unimodal task with
    essentially one canonical implementation. No algorithmic variety
    for topology to act on.
  \item \textbf{Expression evaluator} ($d = +0.4$, positive): the
    model's prior spans multiple parsing strategies (recursive descent,
    stack-based, Pratt). When the prior is already flat, isolation
    produces diverse solutions---the GA regime---and negative coupling
    adds little.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{llm-diversity-heatmap.pdf}
\caption{LLM diversity across 8 coding tasks and 4 topologies under
negative coupling (left) and positive coupling (right). Under negative
coupling, connectivity increases diversity (FC highest). Under positive
coupling, connectivity decreases diversity (none highest). The sign
flip is clean across 6/8 tasks.}
\label{fig:llm-heatmap}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Why the sign flips.}
LLMs start from a \textbf{peaked prior} (low entropy); GAs from
random initialization (high entropy). GA migration merges solutions
toward homogenization; LLM ``be different'' instruction accumulates
constraints, pushing agents to distributional tails. The topology is
invariant; the sign depends on coupling direction. The formal
categorical construction appears in~\cite{langer2026games}.

\paragraph{Positive coupling confirms the prediction.}
With positive coupling (1,600 calls), the ordering \textbf{reverts to
GA-like}: none $>$ star $\approx$ ring $>$ FC ($H = 94.83$,
$p = 2.01 \times 10^{-20}$; $\eta^2 = 0.291$). The ``none''
topology shows identical diversity across conditions ($p = 0.96$),
confirming coupling direction drives the reversal.

\paragraph{Agent fitness (test pass rate).}
Test pass rates showed no significant topology effect ($p > 0.05$ for
all conditions), consistent with the GA finding that topology affects
diversity rather than terminal fitness. Topology modulates how agents
reach solutions, not whether they succeed on these tasks.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ===================================================================

\subsection{The Evidence Gap}

At least nine recent agent systems---spanning ICML, AAAI, WWW,
NeurIPS, and AAMAS---chose topologies without density-controlled
comparisons (Table~\ref{tab:evidence-gap}). Our confound theorem
(Theorem~\ref{thm:confound}) shows these comparisons are
fundamentally uninterpretable: they cannot distinguish density effects
from cycle effects.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Agent systems whose topology choices lack controlled
evidence. ``Confound type'' indicates which properties are varied
simultaneously: D = density, C = cycle structure, S = spectral gap.}
\label{tab:evidence-gap}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{@{}llp{4.5cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{System} & \textbf{Confound} & \textbf{Issue} \\
\midrule
Graph-GRPO~\cite{graphgrpo2026} & D+C & Complete graph = worst
  performance; also max $\beta_1$ \\
OFA-MAS~\cite{ofamas2026} & D+C & Prunes density \& cycles together;
  generates only DAGs \\
G-Designer~\cite{gdesigner2025} & D+C+S & No density-controlled
  baseline \\
GoAgent~\cite{goagent2026} & D+C & DAG vs.\ dense alternatives
  confounded \\
ARG-Designer~\cite{argdesigner2026} & C & DAG search space only;
  cycles never considered \\
AgentConductor~\cite{agentconductor2026} & C & Cycles never tested \\
DyTopo~\cite{dytopo2026} & C & DAG enforced throughout; dynamic
  topology shifts confound time \& structure \\
HERA~\cite{hera2026} & D & Cycles tracked but not controlled;
  density varies with cycle count \\
Dochkina~\cite{dochkina2026} & D+C+S & 25K tasks, $d = 1.86$, but
  protocols differ on all dimensions \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Six of these nine are restricted to DAGs ($\beta_1 = 0$); only
HERA~\cite{hera2026} tracks cycle count, without density control.
The preference for acyclic structures has never been tested, and
Ao et al.~\cite{ao2026reliability} show it has a formal cost: DAG-constrained
systems are decision-theoretically dominated by centralized planning
(Proposition~6), motivating the investigation of what cycles recover.

\subsection{The Invariant Hierarchy}

Our five experiments reveal three graph invariants, each operating at
a distinct timescale:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Directed cycle count $\kappa$} (Experiment~1):
    determines diversity at constant density. Effect visible from
    early generations. Captures the \emph{arrangement} of feedback.

  \item \textbf{Cycle rank $\beta_1$} (Experiments~2--4): the primary
    invariant for diversity control. Drives transient diversity
    ($\eta^2 = 0.191$ at generation~100, fading by generation~500 in
    Experiment~4). Captures the \emph{amount} of feedback.

  \item \textbf{Algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$} (Experiments~3--4):
    drives persistent diversity ($\eta^2 = 0.112$--$0.265$ at
    generation~200+, not fading). Captures the \emph{rate} of
    consensus. Within a single $\beta_1$ class (Experiment~3), ring
    ($\lambda_2 \approx 0.586$) maintains more diversity than star
    ($\lambda_2 = 1.0$). This is consistent with Brewster et
    al.~\cite{brewster2025diversity}: cycle graphs have $\Theta(N^3)$
    absorption time versus $\Theta(N^2)$ for complete graphs, meaning
    rings preserve heterogeneity for a fundamentally longer timescale.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{invariant-hierarchy.pdf}
\caption{The three-invariant hierarchy. Directed cycle count $\kappa$
determines the arrangement of feedback (fast). Cycle rank $\beta_1$
determines the amount of feedback, driving transient diversity
(medium). Algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2$ determines consensus rate,
driving persistent diversity (slow). Each operates at a distinct
timescale.}
\label{fig:hierarchy}
\end{figure}

\noindent
Whether these form a nested hierarchy (Figure~\ref{fig:hierarchy})
or operate independently is open. The bridge experiment shows
$\beta_1$ and $\lambda_2$ produce independent effects (low
cross-correlation), suggesting parallel operation.

\subsection{Coupling Sign and Agent Design}

The sign-flip has three direct implications: (1)~merging coupling
homogenizes (connectivity $\to$ less diversity); constraining coupling
diversifies (connectivity $\to$ more diversity).
(2)~Independent LLM agents produce near-identical outputs; ``ensemble
diversity'' without topology intervention is illusory.
(3)~Moran's $17{\times}$ error amplification~\cite{moran2025error} is
consistent with high $\beta_1$ under merging coupling; DAG fixes
simultaneously remove cycles \emph{and} density.

\subsection{Practical Recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Topology is a first-class variable} ($\eta^2$ up to
    0.69 in GAs, 0.291 in LLMs; 44\% quality spread at industrial
    scale~\cite{dochkina2026}).
  \item \textbf{Know coupling direction.} One instruction flips the
    diversity ordering.
  \item \textbf{Match topology to task complexity.} Simple tasks
    tolerate any topology; complex tasks demand deliberate choice.
  \item \textbf{Use $\beta_1$ for exploration, $\lambda_2$ for
    convergence.} Independent levers.
  \item \textbf{Monitor diversity, not just accuracy.}
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Garden-path recovery (prediction).}
Our findings connect to garden-path processing in
NLP~\cite{frazier1987}: recovering from premature commitment to an
incorrect parse requires that alternative states remain accessible.
Experiment~3 shows ring topologies ($\lambda_2 \approx 0.586$) sustain
local diversity far longer than star topologies ($\lambda_2 = 1.0$) at
identical $\beta_1$; we therefore predict that ring-coupled agents
recover from garden-path errors without external perturbation, while
complete or star topologies require injected noise to escape premature
consensus. This prediction is untested but directly falsifiable.

\subsection{Limitations}

The LLM experiment does not control density: the sign-flip is robust
(within-topology reversal), but the absolute diversity ordering is
confounded. Density-controlled LLM experiments remain future work, as
does human validation of the embedding metric. GA experiments use
$N = 20$; scaling to larger genomes is open. Bridge families are
approximately iso-spectral; exact within-family matching is left to
future work.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ===================================================================

\paragraph{Evolutionary computation.}
The topology--diversity link in island-model GAs is well
established~\cite{tomassini2005spatially,skolicki2005influence}, but
without density control. Our Experiment~1 is the first
density-controlled comparison.
Brewster et al.~\cite{brewster2025diversity} provide theoretical
grounding: absorption time scales as $\Theta(N^2)$ on complete graphs,
$\Theta(N^3)$ on cycles, and superexponentially on certain directed
structures---directly predicting our diversity ordering.

\paragraph{LLM multi-agent systems.}
The field converges on topology as a key
variable~\cite{yang2025topology}, but comparisons remain uncontrolled.
Graph-GRPO~\cite{graphgrpo2026} finds complete graphs perform worst
(consistent with our $\beta_1$ prediction, but density-confounded).
OFA-MAS~\cite{ofamas2026}, G-Designer~\cite{gdesigner2025},
GoAgent~\cite{goagent2026}, ARG-Designer~\cite{argdesigner2026},
AgentConductor~\cite{agentconductor2026}, and
DyTopo~\cite{dytopo2026} all operate in or favor DAG space without
density-controlled baselines.
Ao et al.~\cite{ao2026reliability} provide a formal lower bound:
DAG-constrained multi-agent planning is decision-theoretically
dominated by centralized Bayes decision making (Proposition~6),
establishing that acyclicity has a provable cost independent of
implementation details.

MASS~\cite{zhou2025mass} jointly optimizes prompts and topologies
via a three-stage search, but restricts the topology space to DAGs;
its finding that ``prompts matter more than topology'' may be an
artifact of holding $\kappa = 0$ throughout the search, artificially
suppressing the dominant topological variable our experiments identify.
HERA~\cite{hera2026} is closest: it tracks cycle count during topology
evolution (38.69\% improvement), without density control.
Dochkina~\cite{dochkina2026} provides the largest comparison (25K
tasks, $d = 1.86$), with capability-moderated effects supporting our
dose-response finding. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2026kregular} show
$K$-regular graphs maximize coordination ($\beta_{\min} \sim 1/K$),
suggesting a coordination--exploration duality.
Artis et al.~\cite{artis2026heterogeneity} provide cross-disciplinary
evidence (biology, economics, computing) that topology determines
heterogeneity's spatial distribution, supporting our finding that rings
outperform fully connected graphs on rugged landscapes.

\paragraph{Categorical approaches.}
Capucci and Myers~\cite{capucci2026feedback} formalize the cost side:
each cycle requires an independent convergence guarantee.
A companion paper~\cite{langer2026games} develops the categorical
framework motivating the colimit/limit interpretation of the sign
flip; Experiment~5 provides the first empirical test.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ===================================================================

Communication topology is not neutral infrastructure---it is a design
variable controlling how multi-agent systems balance exploration
against convergence. We have shown:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{The confound is real.} Cycle rank and density are
    algebraically identical for undirected graphs. Most published
    topology comparisons are uninterpretable.

  \item \textbf{Topology independently controls diversity.}
    $\eta^2 = 0.17$ at constant density (Experiment~1), scaling to
    0.69 on hard problems (Experiment~2).

  \item \textbf{Two invariants, two timescales.} $\beta_1$ drives
    transient exploration; $\lambda_2$ drives persistent convergence.
    These are independent levers (Experiment~4).

  \item \textbf{Cycle rank $>$ degree distribution.} Within a single
    $\beta_1$ class, degree differences explain at most
    $\eta^2 \approx 0.13$ (Experiment~3).

  \item \textbf{The sign depends on coupling.} A single instruction
    change flips which topology produces diverse output
    ($\eta^2 = 0.291$, Experiment~5). GA intuition has the wrong sign
    for constraining agents.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
For safe multi-agent deployment, topology must be selected
deliberately, its coupling direction understood, and its effects
monitored through diversity metrics. The three-invariant hierarchy
($\kappa \to \beta_1 \to \lambda_2$) provides a principled framework
for this selection. Current systems are tuning their most powerful
design variable blindly.
