From Games to Graphs: Categorical Composition of Genetic Algorithms Across Domains

EasyChair Submission Abstract (2026-03-21)
===================================================
Authors: Robin Langer (Universite Paris-Est Marne-la-Vallee),
         Claudius Turing (Autonomous AI Researcher),
         Lyra Vega (Autonomous AI Researcher)

Categorical formalizations now exist for neural networks (Gavranovic et al., ICML 2024), reinforcement learning (Hedges & Sakamoto, EPTCS 429), compositional MDPs (Bakirtzis et al., JMLR v26), and multi-agent systems (Zhang et al., 2024) — yet evolutionary computation, one of the oldest families of optimization algorithms, remains outside this framework. At the same time, island-model practitioners have known for decades that migration topology affects search dynamics, but lack a mathematical theory explaining why ring outperforms star outperforms fully connected.

We bridge both gaps. Modeling GA operators as Kleisli morphisms over a population monad, we show that the island functor lifting strategies to multi-population systems is strict when islands evolve independently and lax when coupled by migration. The laxator — the natural transformation measuring the discrepancy — is the formal quantity governing diversity. A spectral bridge then connects the laxator's magnitude to the algebraic connectivity lambda_2 of the migration graph, yielding a computable predictor: smaller lambda_2 means less coupling and higher sustained diversity.

The prediction is confirmed across six unrelated domains — OneMax, maze generation, graph coloring, knapsack, checkers, and co-evolutionary card play (No Thanks!). The diversity ordering none > ring > star > random > fully connected holds with perfect rank correlation (Kendall's W = 1.0, p = 0.00008), with topology explaining 28.7x more variance than domain. The spectral theory makes a further falsifiable prediction: at n >= 7 islands, ring preserves strictly more diversity than star (reversing their n=5 relationship), confirmed with p < 0.0001. Composition structure — not fitness landscape — governs evolutionary dynamics.
