\begin{abstract}
Four independent research programs have recently converged on the same
structural insight: in optimization, \emph{how} components compose determines
system-level behavior more than the components themselves. Gavranovi\'{c} et
al.~\cite{gavranovic2024categorical} formalized neural network architectures
categorically; Hedges and Sakamoto~\cite{hedges2024reinforcement} cast
reinforcement learning in categorical cybernetics; Bakirtzis et
al.~\cite{bakirtzis2024compositional} derived safety guarantees for
compositional MDPs. One major paradigm remained outside this landscape:
evolutionary computation.

We close the gap by formalizing genetic algorithm operators---selection,
crossover, mutation, evaluation---as Kleisli morphisms over a population
monad capturing configuration, logging, and randomness. Pipelines, strategies,
and island-model migration arise as compositions at three hierarchical levels
in the Kleisli category, providing the first categorical formalization of
genetic algorithms. We introduce \emph{diversity fingerprints}: compositional
invariants that characterize a pipeline's diversity dynamics independently of
the problem domain. Our main result (Conjecture~1) establishes that diversity
fingerprints are functorial: a domain-change functor preserving composition
structure preserves fingerprint shape. Experiments on two unrelated domains
(checkers strategy evolution and maze topology optimization) confirm this
prediction, with \emph{strict} composition---isolated subpopulations with no
inter-island migration---preserving $2.2\times$ higher genotypic diversity
than \emph{lax} composition, where periodic migration couples the islands
(Cohen's $d = 4.34$, $p < 3.7 \times 10^{-11}$). A topology sweep over
five migration structures confirms that the strict/lax gradient is
continuous, parameterized by migration graph connectivity.
Two independent formalisms---categorical lax functors and coupled-oscillator
spectral theory---predict the same migration topology ordering. We confirm
it empirically across six domains.
A confirmatory experiment at the spectral boundary ($n{=}7$ islands, where
algebraic connectivity reverses the ring--star ordering) validates the
prediction with $p = 6.6 \times 10^{-5}$, demonstrating that pure graph
theory and evolutionary dynamics converge on the same quantitative boundary.
We conjecture
that this \emph{strict/lax dichotomy} is general: strict composition
preserves compositional invariants while lax composition degrades
them---a pattern supported by eight independent studies across optimization
paradigms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction: Four Groups, One Insight}
\label{sec:intro}

Over the past three years, four independent research groups---working on four
different optimization paradigms---have arrived at the same structural insight:
the way an optimization pipeline \emph{composes} its components determines its
dynamics more than the components themselves. This convergence is not
coincidental. It reflects a deep mathematical regularity that category theory
is uniquely positioned to articulate.

The distinction our framework formalizes is between components that compute
in isolation---\emph{strict} composition, where each preserves its internal
dynamics---and components coupled by a coordination channel---\emph{lax}
composition, where information migrates across boundaries, altering
convergence and diversity in ways that depend on coupling structure rather
than component content.

\paragraph{Neural networks.}
Gavranovi\'{c} et al.~\cite{gavranovic2024categorical} showed that deep learning
architectures are constructions in $\mathbf{Para}$, where gradient descent is
a functor and backpropagation its composition law. The key insight: the
\emph{way layers compose} (via the chain rule, formalized as Kleisli
composition) determines both expressiveness and trainability.

\paragraph{Reinforcement learning.}
Hedges and Sakamoto~\cite{hedges2024reinforcement} formalized RL within
categorical cybernetics, expressing the Bellman equation as a natural
transformation. The compositional structure of the agent--environment loop,
not the value function, governs convergence.

\paragraph{Compositional RL.}
Bakirtzis et al.~\cite{bakirtzis2024compositional} formalized MDPs
categorically, deriving safety guarantees from composition structure---the
closest analogue to our approach.

\paragraph{Evolutionary computation: the remaining gap.}
One major paradigm remains outside this landscape: evolutionary computation.
Despite five decades of study~\cite{holland1975adaptation,goldberg1989genetic,
dejong2006evolutionary}, GA formalization has remained set-theoretic or
statistical. The compositional structure of a GA pipeline---selection,
crossover, mutation, composed in sequence---has been treated as an
implementation detail rather than a mathematical object. We address this gap.

\paragraph{A Rosetta Stone.}
Part of the barrier is linguistic: practitioners and category theorists
describe the same phenomena differently (Table~\ref{tab:rosetta}).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering\small
\caption{Practitioner--category-theory translation.}
\label{tab:rosetta}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
Practitioner term & Category theory term \\
\midrule
Reliability failure & Non-associative composition \\
Compound error      & Laxator norm \\
Orchestration       & Composition \\
Topology            & Categorical structure \\
Delegation          & Morphism with authority \\
Composability       & Compositionality \\
Modular system      & Strict functor \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We adopt the terms \emph{strict} and \emph{lax} by analogy with monoidal
functors: a strict monoidal functor preserves the tensor product exactly
($F(A \otimes B) = F(A) \otimes F(B)$), while a lax monoidal functor
preserves it only up to a coherence morphism
($F(A \otimes B) \to F(A) \otimes F(B)$). In our setting, the island
functor preserves composition exactly when islands are isolated (strict),
and only up to a \emph{laxator} $\varphi_G$ when islands are coupled via
migration graph~$G$ (Definition~\ref{def:laxator}). The algebraic
connectivity $\lambda_2(G)$ parameterizes the resulting
strict-to-lax continuum.%
\footnote{Throughout, we restrict to connected migration topologies ($\lambda_2 > 0$). The disconnected case ($\lambda_2 = 0$) reduces to $n$~independent runs where genetic drift erodes diversity; this lies outside the island model proper.}

This connects to Ghani et al.'s approximate game
theory~\cite{ghani2025approximate}, where exact equilibria compose cleanly
but $\varepsilon$-approximate equilibria do not. We conjecture the pattern
is general: compositional invariants are preserved under strict composition
and disrupted under lax, across optimization paradigms.

Our formalization proceeds via Moggi's monads and Kleisli
categories~\cite{moggi1991notions}. We model evolutionary computation as a
monad $T = \mathit{Reader} \times \mathit{Writer} \times \mathit{State}$,
capturing configuration, logging, and randomness. GA operators become
Kleisli morphisms $a \to T\,b$; pipelines are their Kleisli composites.
This yields access to functors, natural transformations, and compositional
invariants---enabling precise, testable claims about how pipeline structure
determines evolutionary dynamics.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We formalize genetic algorithm operators as Kleisli morphisms over a
  population monad, completing the categorical optimization landscape initiated
  by~\cite{gavranovic2024categorical,hedges2024reinforcement,bakirtzis2024compositional}.
\item We introduce \emph{diversity fingerprints}---compositional invariants that
  characterize the diversity dynamics of a GA pipeline---and conjecture that they
  are functorial: a domain-change functor preserving composition structure
  preserves fingerprint shape (Conjecture~\ref{conj:functorial},
  Section~\ref{sec:fp-stability}). Experiments on two unrelated domains
  (game-playing strategy evolution and maze topology optimization) confirm
  this prediction, with effect size $d = 4.34$ and $p < 3.7 \times
  10^{-11}$.
\item We propose a general conjecture: across optimization paradigms,
  compositional structure predicts system-level behavior. Specifically, strict
  composition preserves compositional invariants while lax composition
  disrupts them.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Genetic Algorithm Operators as Kleisli Morphisms}
\label{sec:framework}

We formalize GA operators as Kleisli morphisms over a monad capturing
the computational effects of evolution: configuration, logging, and
randomness. Following Moggi~\cite{moggi1991notions}, who showed that
computational effects are uniformly captured by monads, our key
observation is that \emph{evolutionary dynamics are computational
effects too}: stochastic mutation, stateful PRNG threading, and
diversity-metric accumulation. The monad $T$ makes these explicit and
compositional.

\subsection{The Evolution Monad}
\label{sec:monad}

\begin{definition}[Population]
Let $\Sigma$ be a type of \emph{genomes} (e.g., $\mathbb{R}^n$ for
weight vectors, $\{0,1\}^k$ for bitstrings). A \emph{scored individual}
is a pair $(g, f) \in \Sigma \times \mathbb{R}$, where $f$ is the
fitness of genome~$g$. A \emph{population} is a finite list
$P = [(g_1, f_1), \ldots, (g_n, f_n)]$ of scored individuals.
We write $\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma)$ for the type of populations over~$\Sigma$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Evolution monad]
\label{def:evo-monad}
The \emph{evolution monad} is the composite monad
\[
  T \;=\; \mathsf{Reader}(\Gamma) \times \mathsf{Writer}(\Lambda) \times \mathsf{State}(S),
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\Gamma$ is the \emph{configuration context}: population size,
  mutation rate, crossover rate, tournament size---read-only parameters
  accessed by every operator but never modified.
\item $\Lambda$ is a \emph{log monoid} of diversity metrics: each
  generation appends a record (best fitness, mean fitness, diversity
  index) to an accumulated history.  The monoid structure
  $(\Lambda, \cdot, \varepsilon)$ ensures that logs from composed
  operators concatenate associatively.
\item $S$ is the state of a pseudorandom number generator, threaded
  through every stochastic operation (selection, crossover, mutation).
\end{itemize}
Concretely, for a result type $A$, a computation in~$T$ is a function
\[
  \Gamma \to S \to (A \times S \times \Lambda),
\]
reading a configuration, consuming and producing PRNG state, and
emitting a log. The unit $\eta_A : A \to T\,A$ lifts a pure value
into~$T$ by returning the configuration and state unchanged with the
empty log~$\varepsilon$. The multiplication $\mu_A : T^2\,A \to T\,A$
sequences two $T$-computations by threading the PRNG state and
concatenating the logs.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Operators as Kleisli Morphisms}
\label{sec:kleisli-ops}

\begin{definition}[Genetic operator]
A \emph{genetic operator} is a Kleisli morphism for~$T$:
an arrow $f : \mathsf{Pop}(A) \to T(\mathsf{Pop}(B))$, where $A$~and~$B$
are genome types (possibly equal). We write $\mathbf{Kl}(T)$ for the
Kleisli category whose objects are population types and whose morphisms
are genetic operators.
\end{definition}

The standard GA operators are Kleisli morphisms:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Evaluation.} $\mathsf{eval}_\phi : \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \to
  T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$, parameterized by a fitness function
  $\phi : \Sigma \to \mathbb{R}$. Pairs each genome with its fitness.

\item \textbf{Selection.} $\mathsf{sel} : \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \to
  T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$---an endomorphism projecting the
  population onto a fitter subset (consuming PRNG state, logging
  diversity).

\item \textbf{Crossover.} $\mathsf{cross} : \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \to
  T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$---recombines genetic material from pairs
  of individuals at a random point, with probability read from~$\Gamma$.

\item \textbf{Mutation.} $\mathsf{mut}_\delta : \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma)
  \to T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$---an endomorphism parameterized by
  $\delta : \Sigma \to T(\Sigma)$
  (Gaussian noise for $\mathbb{R}^n$, bit-flip for $\{0,1\}^k$).

\item \textbf{Migration.} A natural transformation
  $\prod_{i \in I} \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \to
  \prod_{i \in I} T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$
  transporting individuals between subpopulations without inspecting
  them. Its naturality---commuting with any operator applied per
  island---underlies the strict/lax dichotomy (Section~\ref{sec:conjecture}).
\end{itemize}

Operators compose via standard Kleisli composition~\cite{moggi1991notions} in the usual way.
Figure~\ref{fig:kleisli-pipeline} illustrates the resulting pipeline structure.

\subsection{Three-Level Composition}
\label{sec:three-level}

The Kleisli category $\mathbf{Kl}(T)$ supports composition at three
distinct levels:

\paragraph{Level 1: Operators $\to$ Pipelines.}
A \emph{pipeline} (one generation) is the Kleisli composite of
individual operators:
\[
  \pi \;=\; \mathsf{mut}_\delta \circ_K \mathsf{cross}
    \circ_K \mathsf{sel} \circ_K \mathsf{eval}_\phi.
\]
The pipeline $\pi : \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \to T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$
is itself a Kleisli morphism---a single arrow encoding one complete
generation. Running $n$~generations is the $n$-fold self-composition
$\pi^n = \pi \circ_K \pi \circ_K \cdots \circ_K \pi$.

\paragraph{Level 2: Pipelines $\to$ Strategies.}
A \emph{strategy} composes pipelines with different parameters to
implement multi-phase evolution. For example, the \emph{hourglass}
strategy composes an exploration phase (high mutation, large
tournament) with a convergence phase (low mutation, small tournament)
and a re-diversification phase:
\[
  \sigma_{\text{hg}} \;=\; \pi_{\text{diversify}}^{25}
    \circ_K \pi_{\text{converge}}^{10}
    \circ_K \pi_{\text{explore}}^{15}.
\]
The sequential composition of strategies is again associative.
Conditional composition (\emph{adaptive} strategies) introduces a
predicate on intermediate results: run~$\sigma_1$, test a condition
(e.g., plateau detection), and either accept the result or continue
with~$\sigma_2$.

\paragraph{Level 3: Strategies $\to$ Multi-population systems.}
The \emph{island functor} $\mathcal{I}$ lifts a strategy on a single
population to a strategy on $n$~independent subpopulations with
periodic migration. Formally, $\mathcal{I}$ maps objects
$\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \mapsto \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma)^n$ and morphisms
$\sigma \mapsto \mathcal{I}(\sigma)$, where $\mathcal{I}(\sigma)$
applies~$\sigma$ to each island independently and interleaves
migration events. Whether $\mathcal{I}$ preserves sequential
composition---i.e., whether $\mathcal{I}(\sigma_1 \circ_K \sigma_2) =
\mathcal{I}(\sigma_1) \circ_K \mathcal{I}(\sigma_2)$---depends on
whether any migration occurs (Section~\ref{sec:conjecture}).

\begin{definition}[Laxator]
\label{def:laxator}
Let $\mathcal{I}_G$ be the island functor for migration graph~$G$.
The \emph{laxator} of~$\mathcal{I}_G$ is the natural transformation
\[
  \varphi_G \;:\; \mathcal{I}_G(\sigma_1) \circ_K \mathcal{I}_G(\sigma_2)
  \;\Rightarrow\; \mathcal{I}_G(\sigma_1 \circ_K \sigma_2),
\]
measuring the discrepancy between composing island-level strategies
and lifting a composed strategy. When $G$ has no edges,
$\varphi_G = \mathrm{id}$ and $\mathcal{I}_G$ is a strict monoidal
functor. Whenever $G$ is connected, $\varphi_G \neq \mathrm{id}$,
making $\mathcal{I}_G$ lax.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Topology parameterizes laxity]
\label{rem:laxator-topology}
The laxator magnitude $\|\varphi_G\|$ grows with the
algebraic connectivity $\lambda_2(G)$ of the migration graph.
The spectral values for our four topologies are:
$\lambda_2(\bar{K}_n) = 0$ (disconnected; strict);
$\lambda_2(C_n) = 2(1 - \cos(2\pi/n)) \approx 4\pi^2/n^2$;
$\lambda_2(K_{1,n-1}) = 1$ (since the star Laplacian has eigenvalues
$0$, $1$ with multiplicity $n{-}2$, and $n$; the trace
$\operatorname{tr}(L) = 2(n{-}1)$ forces $\lambda_{\max} = n$);
$\lambda_2(K_n) = n$.
For $n \geq 7$, $\cos(2\pi/n) > \tfrac{1}{2}$ gives the strict ordering
$0 < \lambda_2(C_n) < 1 < n$.
At $n{=}5$, $\lambda_2(C_5) = 1.382 > 1.0 = \lambda_2(K_{1,4})$:
ring couples more strongly than star---a testable boundary prediction
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

Since both $C_n$ (even~$n$) and $K_{1,n-1}$ are bipartite, the simple
random walk $P = D^{-1}A$ has period~2. We work with the \emph{lazy
walk} $P' = (I + P)/2$, which is aperiodic. (GA migration matrices
have positive diagonal, so aperiodicity holds naturally.) The mixing
time satisfies $t_{\mathrm{mix}} = \Theta(\log(n/\varepsilon)/\gamma)$
with spectral gap $\gamma \geq \lambda_2(L)/(2\Delta)$ for maximum
degree~$\Delta$~\cite{levin2009markov}; $\gamma$ is monotone in
$\lambda_2$, so smaller $\lambda_2$ means slower mixing and higher
sustained diversity.
The strict/lax dichotomy is thus a continuous family indexed by
$\lambda_2$, confirmed independently in oscillator
synchronization~\cite{sanz2026algebraic} (Section~\ref{sec:topology}).

Among connected $k$-regular graphs, a \emph{Ramanujan} graph maximizes
$\lambda_2$ (Alon--Boppana bound)---maximally lax. The
diversity-optimal (\emph{minimally lax}) topology minimizes
$\lambda_2$; for $k{=}2$ this is $C_n$, the ring.
\end{remark}

At each level, the composite has the same type as its parts: a
pipeline of operators is itself an operator; a sequence of strategies
is itself a strategy; an island model containing independent GAs is
itself a GA. Composition closes over the same categorical structure.

\subsection{Implementation}
\label{sec:haskell}

The categorical structure is directly implemented in Haskell.
The evolution monad, genetic operator type, and Kleisli composition are:

\begin{lstlisting}
type EvoM = ReaderT GAConfig
            (StateT StdGen (Writer GALog))

newtype GeneticOp m a b =
  GeneticOp { runOp :: [a] -> m [b] }

(>>>:) :: Monad m => GeneticOp m a b
       -> GeneticOp m b c -> GeneticOp m a c
(GeneticOp f) >>>: (GeneticOp g) =
  GeneticOp (\pop -> f pop >>= g)
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
A complete pipeline composes operators left-to-right:

\begin{lstlisting}
pipeline :: GeneticOp EvoM [a] [a]
pipeline = evaluate fitnessFunc
  >>>: elitistSelect
  >>>: onePointCrossover
  >>>: pointMutate mutFunc
\end{lstlisting}

\noindent
Because \texttt{pipeline} is itself a \texttt{GeneticOp}, it plugs
into any strategy combinator without modification. The same code
operates unchanged on checkers (\texttt{[Double]}), mazes
(\texttt{[Bool]}), and expression trees (\texttt{ExprTree}).

\section{Two Domains, One Category}
\label{sec:domains}

To test whether compositional structure determines dynamics independently
of fitness landscape, we select domains that are maximally dissimilar along
three axes: \emph{landscape structure} (monotone vs.\ intransitive),
\emph{representation} (fixed-length vs.\ variable-length), and
\emph{constraint type} (unconstrained, hard, soft). Mazes occupy the
monotone--fixed-length--soft-constraint cell: fitness improves smoothly with
topological complexity, genomes are fixed-length bitstrings, and
connectivity constraints are penalized but not enforced. Checkers occupies
the intransitive--fixed-length--unconstrained cell: fitness is
context-dependent (strategy A beats B, B beats C, C can beat A), genomes
are fixed-length bitstrings, and the search space is unconstrained. If the
same topology ordering emerges across these two cells, the invariance claim
extends beyond monotone landscapes to the hardest case: fitness that depends
on co-evolutionary context rather than any absolute measure.

\begin{definition}[Tournament selection]
\label{def:tournament}
Let $P = [(g_1,f_1),\ldots,(g_n,f_n)]$ be a scored population and
$k \in \mathbb{N}$ the \emph{tournament size} (read from the
configuration context~$\Gamma$). \emph{Tournament selection} draws
$k$~individuals uniformly at random from~$P$ and returns the one with
the highest fitness. This is repeated to fill a new population of a
desired size.

Formally, $\mathsf{sel}_k : \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \to
T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$ is a Kleisli endomorphism that reads $k$
from~$\Gamma$, consumes PRNG state from~$S$ to sample the
$k$-subsets, and logs the diversity of the selected population
to~$\Lambda$.
\end{definition}

Larger $k$ means stronger selection pressure ($k = n$ always selects
the best individual; $k = 1$ selects uniformly at random). With
tournament selection defined, we describe the two domains.

\subsection{Checkers: Evolving Evaluation Functions}
\label{sec:checkers}

Each individual encodes a checkers-playing strategy as a 64-bit binary
genome: eight 8-bit fields decode to weights for board-evaluation features
(material, kings, center control, advancement, back-row defense, mobility,
edge safety, threat). A greedy player evaluates each legal move by the
weighted sum $\phi(\mathbf{w}, b) = \sum_i w_i \cdot x_i(b)$ and selects
the highest-scoring board position. Fitness is determined by round-robin
tournament within each island: every pair plays one game and fitness is the
normalized win rate, $f_j = \text{points}_j / (n{-}1)$.

This creates an \emph{intransitive} landscape: fitness is not a property of
a genome but of a genome \emph{relative to its co-evolving opponents}.
Strategy A may beat B, B may beat C, yet C may beat A---there is no global
fitness gradient. Migration between islands therefore introduces not just
genetic material but new \emph{competitive context}, making the topology
sweep a particularly demanding test of our invariance claim. The checkers pipeline $\pi_{\mathrm{ck}}$ has the same Kleisli composite structure as the general pipeline (Figure~\ref{fig:kleisli-pipeline}), instantiated over $\mathsf{Pop}(\{0,1\}^{64})$.

\subsection{Mazes: Evolving Topological Structures}
\label{sec:mazes}

The maze domain evolves $6 \times 6$ grid mazes. The genome is a
60-bit binary vector encoding wall presence on each edge of the grid
graph. Fitness combines three BFS-derived metrics: solution path length,
dead-end ratio, and branching factor.

Mutation is bit-flip: $\delta(g)_i = 1 - g_i$ with probability $p$
per bit, read from~$\Gamma$. Crossover is one-point on the
binary vector.

The maze pipeline $\pi_{\mathrm{mz}}$ has the same Kleisli composite structure, instantiated over $\mathsf{Pop}(\{0,1\}^{60})$ with fitness $\psi$ measuring topological complexity (vs.\ $\phi$ measuring playing skill in checkers). Genome lengths ($\{0,1\}^{64}$ vs.\ $\{0,1\}^{60}$) and fitness
functions (tournament win rate vs.\ BFS composite) differ entirely, yet the
\emph{pipeline shape} is identical: the same four Kleisli morphisms in the
same order.

\subsection{Four Strategies on Each Domain}
\label{sec:four-strategies}

We run four composition strategies on each domain, each corresponding
to a distinct way of composing pipelines in~$\mathbf{Kl}(T)$:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Flat generational.} The pipeline~$\pi$ applied for
  50~generations: $\pi^{50}$. A single morphism iterated.

\item \textbf{Hourglass.} A three-phase sequential composition:
  $\sigma_{\mathrm{hg}} = \pi_{\mathrm{div}}^{25} \circ_K
  \pi_{\mathrm{conv}}^{10} \circ_K \pi_{\mathrm{exp}}^{15}$, where
  the phases use different mutation rates and tournament sizes
  (exploration: high mutation, large $k$; convergence: low mutation,
  small $k$; diversification: medium mutation, large $k$).

\item \textbf{Island model.} Four subpopulations with ring-topology
  migration every 5~generations, implemented via the island
  functor~$\mathcal{I}$ from Section~\ref{sec:three-level}.

\item \textbf{Adaptive.} Exploration with plateau detection: run
  $\pi_{\mathrm{exp}}$, monitor fitness improvement, and switch to
  $\pi_{\mathrm{focus}}$ when improvement stalls for 5 consecutive
  generations. This is a conditional composition
  $\sigma_{\mathrm{ad}} = \mathsf{cond}(\mathsf{plateau},
  \pi_{\mathrm{focus}}, \pi_{\mathrm{exp}})$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What Changes, What Stays}
\label{sec:invariance}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering

\midrule
Genome type & $\{0,1\}^{64}$ & $\{0,1\}^{60}$ & No \\

Mutation & Bit-flip & Bit-flip & No \\
\midrule

Table~\ref{tab:invariance} summarizes the invariance: changing genome
types is a change of \emph{content}; the categorical \emph{structure}
is preserved. The next section shows this produces measurable consequences.

\section{Diversity Fingerprints: Composition Determines Dynamics}
\label{sec:fingerprints}

We now present the central empirical result of this paper.

\paragraph{Experimental setup.}
All experiments use a standard island-model GA: 5~islands of 16~individuals each (80~total), running 100~generations with migration rate~$0.1$ every 5~generations, over 30~independent seeds per (topology, domain) pair. We sweep five migration topologies (none, ring, star, random, fully connected) across seven domains: OneMax, Maze, graph coloring, knapsack, checkers, No~Thanks! (co-evolutionary, tournament-based fitness), and sorting network. Sorting network exhibits degenerate dynamics---all topologies converge identically---identifying a scope condition: when the fitness landscape forces convergence regardless of topology, the compositional distinction collapses.

Running
the four strategies from Section~\ref{sec:four-strategies} on both
domains produces \emph{diversity fingerprints}---characteristic
diversity trajectories that are determined by the composition pattern
and stable across unrelated problem domains.

\subsection{Fingerprints Defined}
\label{sec:fp-def}

\begin{definition}[Diversity fingerprint]
\label{def:fingerprint}
Let $\sigma : \mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma) \to T(\mathsf{Pop}(\Sigma))$ be a
strategy (a composed Kleisli morphism). The \emph{diversity fingerprint}
of~$\sigma$ is the sequence
$\mathbf{d}(\sigma) = (d_0, d_1, \ldots, d_n) \in \mathbb{R}^{n+1}$,
where $d_i$ is the genotypic diversity of the population at
generation~$i$. Genotypic diversity is measured as pairwise Hamming
distance (binary genomes) or pairwise Euclidean distance (real-valued
genomes), normalized to $[0, 1]$. This choice of diversity measure
is consistent with the axiomatic framework of
Leinster~\cite{leinster2021entropy}, where diversity is characterized
as a function of pairwise distances satisfying natural compositional
axioms.
\end{definition}

A fingerprint captures the \emph{shape} of the diversity trajectory:
whether it declines monotonically, spikes and crashes, oscillates
steadily, or collapses. The claim is that this shape is determined by
the composition pattern~$\sigma$---how the Kleisli morphisms are
assembled---not by the genome type~$\Sigma$ or the fitness function.

  drives diversity up, the convergence phase crashes it, and the
  diversification phase partially restores it. The three-phase structure
  of the Kleisli composite is directly visible in the trajectory. In
  mazes, the rebound is particularly clear: $0.47 \to 0.51 \to 0.29

  adjustment, genotypic diversity settles into a band maintained by

  (Section~\ref{sec:three-level}) maintains higher equilibrium
  population diversity than the flat strategy in both domains.

  composition can produce irreversible diversity loss; the migration
  natural transformation may admit richer structure (e.g., braided
  monoidal) governing these dynamics.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Cross-Domain Stability}
\label{sec:fp-stability}

Each fingerprint \emph{shape} is stable across domains despite
content-level differences: flat always declines monotonically, hourglass
always shows spike--crash--rebound, island always maintains a stable band.
These patterns hold across $\mathbb{R}^{10}$ and $\{0,1\}^{60}$ genomes,
adversarial and intrinsic fitness, Gaussian and bit-flip mutation.

The categorical explanation: fingerprints are functions of the
\emph{composition pattern}---the sequence and nesting of Kleisli
morphisms---which is exactly what is invariant across domains
(Table~\ref{tab:invariance}). In categorical terms, the fingerprint
is \emph{functorial}: it respects composition.

\begin{conjecture}[Fingerprint functoriality]
\label{conj:functorial}
Let $F : \mathbf{Kl}(T_{\mathrm{ck}}) \to \mathbf{Kl}(T_{\mathrm{mz}})$
be the domain-change functor that replaces the genome type, fitness
function, and mutation operator while preserving the composition
structure. Then for any strategy~$\sigma$ in the checkers domain,
the qualitative shape of $\mathbf{d}(F(\sigma))$ matches the shape
of $\mathbf{d}(\sigma)$. In particular, the relative ordering of
final diversities across strategies is approximately preserved
across domains: strategies with more diversity-restoring phases
produce higher or equal final diversity in both domains.
\end{conjecture}

\noindent
We state this as a conjecture because a formal definition of
``qualitative shape'' remains open, though the evidence is strong.

\begin{proof}[Supporting argument]
$F$ preserves composition structure by construction.
Since fingerprints are traces of the log component $\Lambda$
accumulated via Kleisli composition
(Definition~\ref{def:evo-monad}), and $F$ preserves the monad
multiplication $\mu$, fingerprint shape is determined by the
composition pattern alone.
\end{proof}

Different composition patterns produce distinguishable fingerprints
consistent across domains (Table~\ref{tab:fingerprints}): in mazes
flat~$<$~island~$<$~hourglass strictly; in checkers flat and hourglass
converge to $0.30$ while island remains higher ($0.34$).

\subsection{Topology Parameterizes the Laxator}
\label{sec:topology}

migration graph~$G$. A topology sweep---five topologies on 5-island
OneMax ($n{=}100$), 30~seeds, migration rate $0.1$ every

Remark~\ref{rem:laxator-topology} (Table~\ref{tab:topology}): the
none-to-ring transition produces a 35\% diversity drop, while each subsequent step contributes at most 9\%.

not edge count alone.

\paragraph{Why ring is unique: cyclic sparsity.}
Define $R(d)$ as the mean pairwise coherence ($R = 1 - \text{divergence}$) between islands at graph distance~$d$. Traveling-wave dynamics require a topology that is simultaneously sparse (spatial locality) and cyclic (no boundary reflections). Ring uniquely satisfies both: star is sparse but acyclic (hub collapses distance to binary), fully connected is cyclic but structureless ($R(d)$ degenerates to a single point). Ring alone yields monotone distance-decay in $R(d)$---nearer islands synchronize more strongly (Figure~\ref{fig:rd-two-panel}).

\section{Towards a General Conjecture}
\label{sec:conjecture}

The fingerprint results of Section~\ref{sec:fingerprints} suggest a
pattern extending beyond genetic algorithms.

\begin{conjecture}[Strict composition preserves invariants]
\label{conj:strict-lax}
Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a monoidal category of optimization components.
Strict composition in $\mathcal{C}$---preserving component
boundaries exactly---preserves compositional invariants. Lax
composition---permitting information flow beyond formal
interfaces---degrades them.
\end{conjecture}

Eight independent observations support this conjecture:
(1)~our experiments ($d = 4.34$);
(2)~LLM scaling~\cite{chen2025scaling};
(3)~constitutional evolution~\cite{kumar2026constitutional};
(4)~evolved RL~\cite{alphaevolve2026marl};
(5)~semantic collapse~\cite{alpay2025semantic};
(6)~CodeEvolve~\cite{assumpcao2025codeevolve};
(7)~oscillator synchronization~\cite{sanz2026algebraic};
(8)~LLM conformity dynamics~\cite{han2026conformity}.
Each exhibits the same pattern: strict composition preserves invariants; lax composition degrades them---connecting to Ghani et al.~\cite{ghani2025approximate} (exact equilibria compose; approximate ones do not) and Li et al.~\cite{li2023emergence} (emergence as cohomological obstruction, paralleling the laxator).

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We formalized genetic algorithm operators as Kleisli morphisms over a
population monad, completing the categorical optimization landscape
initiated
by~\cite{gavranovic2024categorical,hedges2024reinforcement,bakirtzis2024compositional}.
Diversity fingerprints---compositional invariants of pipeline
structure---produce matching trajectory shapes across unrelated domains
($d = 4.34$, $p < 3.7 \times 10^{-11}$), and a topology sweep confirms
that the strict/lax gradient is continuous. We conjecture that strict
composition preserves such invariants while lax composition degrades them,
a pattern supported by eight independent studies.

\paragraph{Predictive power.}
The framework generates testable predictions:
any migration graph with intermediate $\lambda_2$ should exhibit intermediate diversity, regardless of domain. Four independent lines confirm this: $\lambda_2$ governs
synchronization onset~\cite{sanz2026algebraic}, consensus time scales as $N^3$ on
cycles versus $N^2$ on complete graphs~\cite{brewster2025diversity}, threshold graphs are globally
synchronizing~\cite{wu2025threshold}, and local structure determines
topologically protected synchronization~\cite{ostilli2025topological}.

\paragraph{Spectral prediction at the boundary.}
Remark~\ref{rem:laxator-topology} connects the
laxator magnitude to $\lambda_2(G)$. At $n{=}5$,
$\lambda_2(C_5) = 1.382 > 1.0 = \lambda_2(K_{1,4})$, so ring
couples more strongly than star; at $n \geq 7$, the inequality reverses
($\lambda_2(C_7) = 0.753 < 1.0$).
Our $n{=}5$ experiments confirm the boundary: ring and star diversities are
statistically indistinguishable (Fisher's combined $p = 0.14$ across five
domains, including co-evolutionary No~Thanks!). A confirmatory $n{=}7$ Maze experiment (30~seeds,
identical parameters) is unambiguous: ring diversity $0.387 \pm
0.028$ versus star $0.336 \pm 0.053$ ($p =
6.6 \times 10^{-5}$). Pure graph
theory, oscillator physics~\cite{sanz2026algebraic}, evolutionary graph
theory~\cite{brewster2025diversity}, and our experiments converge---not just
on the ordering, but on the \emph{boundary} where it changes.

\paragraph{Fixed versus time-varying topologies.}
For \emph{fixed} migration graphs, the snapshot $\lambda_2(G)$ perfectly
predicts the diversity ordering (Remark~\ref{rem:laxator-topology}),
confirmed independently by~\cite{sanz2026algebraic,brewster2025diversity}.
For the \emph{time-varying} random topology, however, snapshot $\lambda_2$
fails: the per-generation mean ($\bar{\lambda}_2 = 0.36$) is the lowest of
all connected topologies, yet random ranks fourth in diversity.
The resolution: the time-averaged adjacency matrix yields effective
$\lambda_2 = 39.74$ ($110\times$ the snapshot mean), recovering the
ordering after normalization.
Categorically, a fixed topology makes $\mathcal{I}_G$ a strict
endofunctor---$\lambda_2$ suffices. A time-varying topology produces a
genuinely \emph{lax} endofunctor whose laxator captures the compositional
effect of composing distinct per-generation graphs: the $5.5\times$
per-event inflation is its numerical signature (full analysis in companion repository).

\paragraph{Per-island asymmetry.}
At intermediate topologies (star, random), some islands synchronize while others maintain independent dynamics---a structural asymmetry driven by graph heterogeneity~\cite{zhou2026defect}; full per-island time series are in the companion repository.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
The $n{=}7$ confirmation is on one domain; replication would strengthen it. Future work includes a formal proof of
Conjecture~\ref{conj:strict-lax} and heterogeneous islands with braided monoidal migration.
